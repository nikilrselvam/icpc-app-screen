## ICPC Questions

#### Why ACM ICPC?
>For much of my childhood, I saw mathematics and computer science as largely mutually exclusive, and didn’t bother to learn anything about programming. When a friend suggested I check out Project Euler, I picked up a little Python to solve the first few problems and was immediately hooked.
>
>I spent a genuinely unhealthy amount of my free time in 12th grade just solving Project Euler problems and learning to optimise my solutions. I was a little eccentric about not using any libraries to optimise my code and, to a large degree, writing up everything I needed to solve a problem from scratch, and so my favourite thing was to improve upon my algorithm for generating prime numbers. While my progress (in terms of problems solved) was slow — for each hour I spent writing a working solution, I spent five more optimising it and trying to get every last drop of performance possible — I developed a solid foundation in programming I didn’t then think would lead anywhere outside of Project Euler.
>
>When I came to college and discovered competitive programming, I tried out for the ACM ICPC team my very first quarter. After two hours of trying to complete the Kattis contest, though, I realised my knowledge of computer science had huge holes for the lack of any formal education in it, and decided to take try out again after taking CS 31.
>
>A couple quarters later, even after I had taken CS 31 and 32, I hesitated to try out for ICPC for fear I wouldn’t do much better than I did in that first try-out, where I couldn’t solve a single problem and only in part because of trouble with I/O. I was also working 20 hours a week and taking 4-5 classes each quarter to make an early graduation possible. Not much has changed in that respect, except that entering my last year at UCLA, a year I’ll likely spend fully away from campus, has given me perspective on what’s important to me. I genuinely love solving computational problems, and I’d be remiss if I didn’t even try to involve myself in ICPC and learn from other people who are interested in the same thing and really good at it too. The workshops I’ve attended these past few weeks have also been nothing but encouraging; while I don’t know if I’ll get a chance to compete before I graduate, I do certainly want to work for ICPC and maybe help make that possible for other people.

#### What’s your favorite problem? It does not have to be an algorithm, or even CS related!
>The two-language problem! I fully admit recency bias is half the reason this is my answer, and the other half is that I’ve finally discovered (what I think is) a solution to this problem in the Julia programming language, but it’s something I’ve grappled with since I first started programming.
>
>Python was my first language of choice because I was then more interested in solving Project Euler problems than I was in learning programming, and it took minimal effort to pick up and work with. As I started doing some of the more challenging problems though, I found myself woefully limited by how slow it was and reluctant to pick up a faster language that would increase my prototyping time. After learning C++ in CS 31 and CS 32 (and foundational computer science, of course), I started solving Project Euler problems in the much faster language but quite missed using Python for how much easier it used to be to right code in. After a little bit of research over the summer about this problem, I learned it’s a common version of what’s called the two-language problem, and discovered Julia, a supposed solution to it — a programming language as high-level as Python and far closer to C/C++ in performance.
>
>I’ve been programming in Julia for the past couple of months now, and learning the language (and its own shortcomings) has helped develop special appreciation for the two-language problem. Through the Julia community, I’ve learned about and grown more interested in the more technical aspects of writing performant code, and now keenly follow how different programming paradigms grow to address the two-language problem.
