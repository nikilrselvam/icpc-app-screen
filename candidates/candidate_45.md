## ICPC Questions

#### Why ACM ICPC?
>Since the days of hunting for Easter eggs as an infant, I’ve been competitive out of the womb. Throughout school, I always found myself involved with competitions, whether it be Math Olympiad, hackathons, or even USACO. When it comes to training for events throughout the year, ICPC appeals heavily to the side of me that’s always looking for a competition.
>On top of just competing, I’ve also had an appreciation for the beauty of algorithms. Specifically, seeing a cleanly presented solution to a problem is always extremely satisfying to me, and I’m inclined to learn that formula. Since I’m currently not taking a computer science class, ICPC provides me with my fair share of algorithms.
>Of course, I wouldn’t have any of the knowledge I currently have (both in CS and beyond) if it weren’t for amazing teachers and mentors. Thus, I’m always looking for ways to pay it forward by teaching myself. Seeing the ICPC officers dedicate their time each week towards teaching a range of skill levels makes me want to continue that legacy and mentor newer students getting into competitive programming or just CS as a whole.

#### What’s your favorite problem? It does not have to be an algorithm, or even CS related!
>My favorite problem would have to be printing out the nth Fibonacci number. This problem seemed so simple to me when I was just starting out CS; just write a recursive function and you’re done right? Wrong! After seeing the stack overflow because of too many recursive calls, I was taught to store intermediate values along the way, which instantly streamlined the solution. Yet even then, another solution would be to calculate from bottom-up, saving memory from stored intermediate values. I was reminded of the subtle complexities of this problem when recently exposed to the similar problem of distinct ways to climb n steps at the ICPC Coding Interview Crash Course. This prompted me to continue thinking about what type of problems I could reduce down to a sequence as such, hopefully netting me a solution or two in future interviews.

#### Do you have any past experience with teaching? (It is not required that you do!)
>Starting freshman year of high school, I began teaching Java for Silicon Valley Youth, a local non-profit. I created my own syllabus and adjusted it according to the other levels of CS taught at SVY. Every semester, I ran classes for around 15 middle school students covering basic topics like variables all the way up to recursion. While students came in at varying levels, I was able to gear the class specifically towards each student by providing both easy and challenge problems. Through balancing class concepts, I made sure that by the end of my class, students would leave as competent coders.
